# Problem Selection

Guidelines for choosing what to work on. Read this during `/brainstorm` and `/reassess`.

## What makes a great problem

A great problem is one where the answer changes how people think or act. Not all publishable problems are great. Apply these filters in order:

### 1. Does anyone care?
If you solve this, who benefits and what do they do differently? "Advances our understanding of X" is not a great answer. "Enables experimentalists to do Y in hours instead of weeks" is. If the answer is "other people who work on this specific method" — the problem is too narrow.

### 2. Is there a conceptual gap, not just a knowledge gap?
"We don't know X" is weak. "We don't understand *why* X" or "the current framework *cannot* handle X" is strong. The best problems reveal that something people assumed was true is actually wrong, or that something thought to be hard is actually easy (or vice versa).

### 3. Is the timing right?
A great problem at the wrong time is a frustrating problem. The pieces need to exist: the theory, the tools, the data, or the experimental platform. Look for problems where recent advances (new platform, new algorithm, new dataset) have *just* made something tractable that wasn't before.

### 4. Is it a natural problem or a manufactured one?
Natural problems arise from genuine obstacles people face. Manufactured problems are created to fill a gap in a paper. If you have to explain why anyone would care, the problem is probably manufactured. The best problems are ones practitioners already have but haven't formalised.

### 5. Does it connect fields?
Problems that sit at the intersection of two fields — where each field has a piece but neither has the whole picture — tend to produce surprising results. The synthesis is the contribution.

### 6. Can you state it in one sentence?
If the problem takes a paragraph to state, it's not crisp enough. Refine until the core question fits in one sentence that a non-expert could understand.

## Red flags

- The main contribution is "we applied method X to domain Y" with no new insight
- The problem only exists because of a specific modelling choice, not because of reality
- You need more than 2 sentences to explain why it matters
- The expected result is obvious — what would be *surprising*?
- No one has worked on this, but not because it's hard — because it's not interesting
