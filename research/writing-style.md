# Writing Style

Follow these when writing or editing papers and technical prose.

## Story Structure

- Everything follows the **OCAR arc**: Opening (what we know) → Challenge (the gap) → Action (what we did) → Resolution (what we learned and why it matters).
- The challenge must fit in one sentence. The resolution must answer it directly.
- The opening must make the reader care before describing what was done. Establish significance in the world, not just the subfield.
- Prefer **conceptual gaps** (we don't understand *why* X) over knowledge gaps (we don't know X) — they create stronger motivation.

## Structure at Every Scale

- OCAR applies fractally: whole paper, each section, each paragraph.
- First sentence of every paragraph = the point. Everything after = support. Reading only first sentences should give the complete argument.
- Every sentence, paragraph, and section must advance the argument. No passengers — if removing it doesn't break the chain, cut it.
- Old before new: start each sentence with what the reader knows, end with the new information.
- Use logical connectives ("however", "because", "therefore") to make relationships explicit, but don't use filler transitions ("moreover", "furthermore", "additionally") as crutches.

## Sentences

- Make the agent and action obvious. Keep subject and verb close together.
- Active voice by default. Passive only when the agent is unknown, irrelevant, or emphasis must shift.
- Positive form: "He usually arrived late" not "He was not very often on time."
- Parallel construction for items in series and coordinate clauses.
- Short sentences carry weight. Length ≠ sophistication.
- Every sentence should stand on its own. If it's parasitic on the previous one, strengthen or merge it.

## Word Choice and Clutter

- Omit needless words. "The question as to whether" → "whether". "The reason why is that" → "because".
- Delete all clutter: "the fact that", "in terms of", "at this point in time", "it is interesting to note that".
- Kill nominalizations: "provide a description" → "describe". "the implementation of" → "implementing".
- Kill hedging pileups: "it could perhaps be argued that" → state the claim or don't.
- Kill meta-discourse: "In this section, we discuss…" → just discuss it.
- Short familiar words: "use" not "utilise", "show" not "demonstrate", "help" not "facilitate".
- Remove weak qualifiers: "a bit", "sort of", "somewhat". Commit or don't.
- Concrete before abstract. Ground in specifics, then generalise. Abstractions need anchors.
- No automatic phrases: "it goes without saying", "needless to say", "at the end of the day".
- Avoid too many "--" and ";".

## Clarity and Audience

- Clear ≠ dumbed-down. Make complex ideas accessible; don't make simple ideas sound complex.
- **Classic style**: position the reader to see what you see. Prose is a window, not a mirror.
- Guard against the **curse of knowledge**: you forget what the reader doesn't know. Always ask: would someone outside my head follow this?
- Match framing to the audience: jargon level, opening breadth, assumed background.
- Write as one human speaking to another. If you wouldn't say it aloud, don't write it.

## Papers That Get Cited

- Frame the contribution as a shift in understanding, not just new data.
- **Title**: convey the finding, not just the topic.
- **Abstract**: standalone OCAR mini-story. Don't waste space on methods unless the method is the contribution.
- **Introduction**: an argument, not a literature review. Funnel: broad significance → specific gap → your question. Every citation serves the argument.
- **Discussion**: don't recapitulate results. Interpret, connect to literature, address limitations, state the new understanding. End with the "so what?" — zoom back to broad significance.
- **Figures**: each carries a clear message. Captions should be interpretable without the main text.

## Revision

- Rewriting is writing. First drafts get ideas down; revision makes every sentence earn its place.
- Resist explaining: if "What I mean is…" follows, the first sentence wasn't good enough. Fix it.
- Don't overwrite. Don't pile adjectives and adverbs.
- Maintain unity of pronoun, tense, and tone throughout.
- End strong. The last sentence of any passage carries disproportionate weight.

## Checklist

- [ ] Opening creates a reason to keep reading
- [ ] Single, clearly stated challenge; resolution answers it
- [ ] Every paragraph advances the argument; first sentences give the full arc
- [ ] Subject and verb obvious and close in every sentence
- [ ] No sentence requires re-reading
- [ ] Clutter, qualifiers, nominalizations, and hedge phrases removed
- [ ] Concrete examples anchor abstract claims
- [ ] Framing matches the target reader; jargon eliminated or defined
- [ ] Title conveys finding
- [ ] Ending is strong and closes the arc opened at the start
